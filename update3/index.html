Major Planets

On this web page my goal is to help provide some additional imformation about the different planets in our solar system. The major planets in our solar system are divided into two categories: the inner, rocky planets and the outer, gas giants. The inner planets—Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars—are composed mostly of rock and metal and have relatively small, dense structures. Mercury is the closest to the Sun and has extreme temperature fluctuations, while Venus is known for its thick, toxic atmosphere and surface temperatures hot enough to melt lead. Earth, the only planet known to support life, has a diverse environment with liquid water, and Mars, with its red surface and large volcanoes, is a key focus for exploration, as it may have once harbored life. The outer planets—Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune—are much larger and are primarily composed of gases, with thick atmospheres made mostly of hydrogen and helium. Jupiter, the largest planet, has a famous storm system known as the Great Red Spot, and Saturn is famous for its expansive and striking ring system. Uranus and Neptune, often called the "ice giants," have colder, methane-rich atmospheres and unique axial tilts. These outer planets also have many moons, some of which, like Jupiter's Europa or Saturn's Titan, are of particular interest to scientists studying the possibility of life beyond Earth.
